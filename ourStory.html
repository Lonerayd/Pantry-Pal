Our Story

Filipino food history is rich and diverse, with influences from various cultures and regions. Luzon, Visayas, and Mindanao are the three main geographical regions of the Philippines, and each has its unique culinary traditions. Here's an overview of the food history from each region: Luzon:

  • Pre-colonial Era: Before the arrival of the Spaniards, Luzon's cuisine consisted of indigenous ingredients like rice, root crops, fish, and game meats. Fermentation techniques were used to preserve food.
  • Spanish Influence: The Spanish colonization greatly influenced Luzon's food. Spanish ingredients such as tomatoes, garlic, onions, and various spices were introduced. Iconic dishes like adobo (meat marinated in vinegar and soy sauce) and cocido (stew) have Spanish roots.
  • Chinese Influence: The Chinese traders and immigrants brought their culinary traditions, such as stir-frying techniques and noodles. Pancit (noodles) and lumpia (spring rolls) are popular examples of Chinese-influenced dishes.
  • Regional Specialties: Luzon has diverse regional specialties. Pampanga is known for its rich and flavorful dishes like sisig (chopped pork face and liver) and bringhe (a variation of paella). Ilocos region is famous for its bagnet (deep-fried crispy pork) and empanada (stuffed pastry).

    • Visayas:

  • Indigenous Cuisine: The Visayan islands have a rich history of indigenous culinary traditions. Seafood, coconut, and root crops like cassava and taro are commonly used ingredients. The Visayans have traditional cooking methods such as inun-unan (fish cooked in vinegar) and binakol (chicken soup cooked with coconut water).
  • Spanish Influence: Like Luzon, the Spanish colonization influenced Visayan cuisine. Dishes like lechon (roast pig) and estofado (stewed meat) were introduced and became popular.
  • Festive Delicacies: Visayas is known for its festive delicacies. In Cebu, lechon is especially famous, often considered one of the best in the country. In Negros Occidental, chicken inasal (grilled chicken marinated in local spices) is a favorite dish.
  • Seafood Delights: Given its island geography, Visayas offers an abundance of fresh seafood dishes. Kinilaw (ceviche) and sinuglaw (grilled pork and fish ceviche) are popular seafood preparations.

    • Mindanao:

  • Indigenous Muslim Cuisine: Mindanao is home to various indigenous Muslim communities, and their cuisine reflects their cultural traditions. Dishes like piaparan (chicken cooked in turmeric) and tiyula itum (black soup made with burnt coconut) are unique to Mindanao.
  • Malay Influence: Mindanao has a strong Malay influence, seen in dishes like lansiao (beef soup with ginger) and pastil (rice topped with shredded chicken or beef).
  • Fruit and Spices: Mindanao's tropical climate allows for the cultivation of fruits like durian, pomelo, and mangosteen. Spices such as turmeric, ginger, and chili peppers are commonly used in their dishes.
  • Fusion Cuisine: Mindanao's proximity to other Southeast Asian countries and its history of trade has led to a fusion of culinary influences. Dishes like beef rendang (spicy Indonesian beef stew) and satay (grilled skewered meat) can be found in Mindanao.

    • It's important to note that this overview provides a general perspective, and there are countless more dishes and culinary traditions within each region. Filipino food history is incredibly diverse, reflecting the country's complex history.
